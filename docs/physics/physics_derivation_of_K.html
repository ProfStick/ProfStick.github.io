Deriving the equation for Kinetic Energy

Kinetic energy is the energy an object possesses due to its motion. We can derive the equation for kinetic energy by considering how much energy Albert would gain if Prof Stick pulled him on his frictionless skateboard from rest, with a constant force FFF over a distance sss so that he accelerates to some velocity vvv.

Initially, Albert is not moving so has no kinetic energy, and because he lives in an ideal universe with frictionless skateboards, all the work (energy) that Prof Stick does on him is converted into kinetic energy:

ΔK0→v=Fs\Delta K_{0 \rightarrow v} = F sΔK0→v​=Fs(1)

where ΔK0→v\Delta K_{0 \rightarrow v}ΔK0→v​ is the gain in kinetic energy as Albert goes from rest to velocity vvv.Applying Newton's Second Law gives

ΔK0→v=mas\Delta K_{0 \rightarrow v} = m a sΔK0→v​=mas(2)

If the force is constant, then the acceleration will be constant and we can use the suvatsuvatsuvat equations. These tell us that v2=u2+2asv^2 = u^2 + 2 a sv2=u2+2as. Because Albert was initially at rest, we can simplify this to v2=2asv^2 = 2 a sv2=2as which we can rearrange to:

2as=v22 a s = v^22as=v2

 

s=v22as = \dfrac{v^2}{2 a}s=2av2​(3)

If we substitute (3) into (2) we get:

ΔK0→v=mav22a\Delta K_{0 \rightarrow v} = m a \dfrac{v^2}{2 a}ΔK0→v​=ma2av2​

 

ΔK0→v=12mv2\Delta K_{0 \rightarrow v} = \frac{1}{2} m v^2ΔK0→v​=21​mv2(4)

 

We were able to derive equation (4) because Albert started from rest and Prof Stick applied a constant force. However, our definition of kinetic energy is the energy an object possesses due to its motion, and it doesn't matter how an object achieves that motion, the energy should be the same. So we can generalise the kinetic energy equation to:

K=12mv2K = \frac{1}{2} m v^2K=21​mv2

and we're done!
